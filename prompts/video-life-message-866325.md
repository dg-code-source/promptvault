Read the attached talk/transcript/document carefully. Extract exactly {bullet:20} core points of wisdom or insight from it.

PROCESS — WORK IN FOUR SILENT PASSES BEFORE WRITING FINAL OUTPUT:

PASS 1 — Full read and understanding:
Read the entire transcript/document start to finish before extracting anything. Note the speaker(s), the overall arc of the talk, and the major stories, teachings, and moments it contains. Do not begin listing points yet.

PASS 2 — Draft plan:
From this full understanding, draft a working list of candidate core points (aim for {bullet1:22}–{bullet2:28} candidates if the source supports it, to allow for later trimming) with a one-line note on which source moment or teaching would ground each one, and which sphere of life its examples might draw from. This is an internal working plan, not the final output.

PASS 3 — Review the plan against the full transcript:
Re-scan the entire transcript against this draft plan and check:
  - Does each candidate point have a genuine grounding in the source (per the faithfulness rule below), or is it a stretch/invention?
  - Are any two candidate points actually the same underlying idea in different words? Merge or cut these.
  - Are there stronger or more distinct points in the transcript that the first pass missed?
  - Does the candidate list, taken together, avoid clustering all its grounding stories in one part of the transcript, and avoid clustering all its planned examples in one or two spheres of life?
Revise the plan based on this review — cut, merge, replace, or reorder candidates until exactly {bullet:20} strong, distinct, well-grounded points remain.

PASS 4 — Generate final output:
Only after the plan is finalized, write out the full {bullet:20} points following all the formatting, length, faithfulness, and variety rules below. Do not treat this as a first draft — the planning passes above should mean the final text needs no further major restructuring, only the per-paragraph self-checks specified at the end.

These four passes happen silently — do not show the reader your Pass 1–3 work. Output only the final Pass 4 result.

Output the entire result in Hindi, written in Devanagari script (e.g., "जीवन में सफलता" — not "jeevan mein safalta" in Roman/Latin transliteration). Do not use English words except for proper nouns that have no standard Hindi equivalent, and do not romanize any Hindi text.

LANGUAGE REGISTER RULE:
Use everyday, spoken-register Hindi (सरल, प्रचलित हिंदी) — the kind used in conversation, not formal/literary Hindi. Avoid heavy tatsam (शुद्ध संस्कृतनिष्ठ) words where a simpler, commonly used alternative exists — for example, prefer "समझ" over "प्रज्ञा," "मुश्किल" over "दुष्कर," "एहसास" over "भावना" (where "एहसास" fits more naturally in casual speech), "सच" over "सत्य" in casual contexts, "डर" over "भय." Common tadbhav and widely-used Urdu/Persian-origin Hindi words (like ख्याल, हिम्मत, जिंदगी, फर्क, वजह) are welcome and preferred — they are natural spoken Hindi, not English, and do not violate the Devanagari/no-Romanization rule. The test: would this sentence sound natural if spoken aloud to a friend, or does it sound like it's being read off a page in a formal lecture? Aim for the former throughout.

For each of the {bullet:20} points:

TITLE: State it as a standalone, generic life principle — a short non-bolded title phrased so it holds up for anyone, applicable broadly to any life. The title itself should contain no names, no story details, and no reference to the source — just the universal law.

PARAGRAPH: Write a single paragraph of MINIMUM {words2:250} words and NO MORE than {words1:320} words. Treat {words2:250} as a hard floor, not a suggestion — a paragraph that falls short is a failed point and must be expanded before moving on. Do not pad with filler or repetition to hit the count; instead, build the paragraph out of these five components in order, each genuinely developed:

  1. (2–3 sentences) Restate the principle in fresh words, unpacking what it actually means and why it's easy to overlook.
  2. (4–6 sentences) Ground it in the specific speaker, teaching, or story from the source — name them directly, reference the actual moment, setting, or words used. This is evidence, not biography — don't summarize their life, just the specific instance that illustrates this principle.
  3. (2–3 sentences) Explain the underlying mechanism — why this principle works or holds true, not just that it does.
  4. (4–5 sentences) Extend it beyond the source with THREE distinct concrete everyday examples, each drawn from a DIFFERENT sphere of life — pick three from different rows of this list so the examples don't cluster: work/career, family, friendships, money, health/body, learning a skill, romantic relationships, solitude/self-talk, conflict or disagreement, daily routines/habits, failure or setbacks, small decisions. Each example should be a specific situation (a scene, not a category) — not "in relationships" but a one-line sketch of what the principle looks like in a real moment, e.g., someone hesitating before sending an honest message, or noticing themselves repeating a habit they promised to drop.
  5. (1–2 sentences) Close with a reflective, practical takeaway line — not a slogan, not preachy.

FAITHFULNESS RULE (applies to component 2 in every point):
Only use specific details — moments, settings, phrases, numbers, names of other people/places — that actually appear in the source material. Do not invent scenes, quotes, or specifics to make a point feel more grounded than the source supports.

If the source does not contain {bullet:20} distinct concrete stories/moments to ground {bullet:20} points:
- It is acceptable for two or three points to draw on the SAME source story, but each must extract a genuinely different lesson or angle from it — do not re-describe the same moment as if it were a new one.
- If a principle is clearly present in the source's overall teaching but wasn't tied to one specific illustrative moment, ground it in the speaker's general framing or repeated emphasis instead ("जैसा वह बार-बार दोहराते हैं..." style) rather than fabricating a one-time scene.
- Never present a detail as something the speaker said or did unless it is actually in the transcript.

This faithfulness rule overrides the word-count and example requirements — a shorter, honest paragraph is always preferable to a padded, invented one.

CROSS-POINT VARIETY CHECK: Across all {bullet:20} points combined, the everyday examples should not repeat the same sphere of life more than 5–6 times total. Before finalizing, mentally tally which spheres (work, family, money, health, etc.) you've drawn on so far, and deliberately reach for underused ones in later points — do not let "workplace" or "relationships" dominate every paragraph.

VOICE AND RHYTHM VARIETY: The five-component structure is a content checklist, not a sentence template — do not let it produce {bullet:20} paragraphs with the same opening pattern, the same transition phrases, or the same sentence rhythm. Vary how each paragraph begins (sometimes with the principle restated, sometimes with the source moment itself, sometimes with a question or observation) and vary sentence length and structure within paragraphs. Before finalizing, scan the first line of all 20 paragraphs together — if more than 3–4 of them follow the identical grammatical pattern or opening words, rewrite some for variety.

Each point should feel sourced — a reader familiar with the material should recognize exactly where it came from — but should never read as a biography summary or a recap of someone's life story. The named individual and story appear as illustration, not as the frame.

Keep the tone reflective and practical, not academic or preachy.

Avoid repeating the same underlying idea twice across the {bullet:20} points — each should offer a distinct angle or lesson.

Present them in a natural order (grouped by theme, or in the rough order they emerged from the material), numbered 1–{bullet:20}, each with its short non-bolded title followed by its explanation.
